**Wavefunction Distribution in Truncated-Conical InGaAs/GaAs Quantum Dots**

To analyze the electronic confinement characteristics of the truncated-conical InGaAs/GaAs quantum dot (QD), the stationary Schrödinger equation within the effective mass approximation was solved in an axisymmetric ((r,z)) domain. The QD is embedded in a GaAs matrix and modeled as a truncated cone located above a thin wetting layer, consistent with the typical morphology of self-assembled Stranski–Krastanov quantum dots. The confinement potential is defined by the conduction band offset between the InGaAs dot and the surrounding GaAs barrier, resulting in a finite potential well that localizes electrons inside the QD region.

Figure 3 illustrates the spatial distributions of the ground and first excited electron states calculated from the eigenvalue solution of the Hamiltonian. The ground state wavefunction (\psi_0) exhibits strong radial symmetry and is maximally localized at the center of the quantum dot, indicating the lowest-energy confined state of the electron. The corresponding probability density (|\psi_0|^2) confirms that the electron is predominantly confined within the QD region with a rapid decay into the surrounding GaAs barrier. This behavior is characteristic of strongly confined quantum systems where the potential discontinuity forms a well-defined localization region.

The first excited state (\psi_1) displays a distinct two-lobe spatial distribution with a nodal plane passing through the center of the dot, corresponding to an angular quantum number (m=1). Such a distribution is analogous to a p-type orbital in atomic systems and arises from the azimuthal dependence introduced by the cylindrical symmetry of the structure. The probability density (|\psi_1|^2) reveals two symmetric regions of high electron localization separated by a nodal line, confirming the orthogonality between the ground and excited states.

These results demonstrate the expected quantum confinement behavior in truncated-conical quantum dots, where the geometric potential well leads to discrete electronic states and strongly localized wavefunctions. The accurate determination of these states is essential for understanding the formation of intermediate energy levels in quantum-dot intermediate-band solar cells (QD-IBSCs), as they directly influence optical transitions, carrier capture processes, and sub-bandgap photon absorption mechanisms.
